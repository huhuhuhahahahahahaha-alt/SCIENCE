Section 06

Analysis & Conclusion

Comparing the two cases reveals a direct inverse relationship. When the radius increases from 23 m to 97 m — a factor of approximately 4.22× — the centripetal acceleration decreases by the same factor, from 4.3478 m/s² to 1.0309 m/s².

aᶜ ∝ 1/r
when speed v is held constant

A larger radius means a gentler curve, so the car needs less centripetal acceleration to stay on track at the same speed. Conversely, a tight curve (small r) demands far greater inward acceleration — and therefore much greater centripetal force from the tyres.

This is why race cars slow down for hairpin bends: the required centripetal force at high speed and small radius can exceed the friction limit of the tyres.
